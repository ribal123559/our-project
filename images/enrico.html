About Enrico Fermi

Enrico Fermi (1901-1954) was an Italian-American physicist, known for his work on the development of the first nuclear reactor and his contributions to the development of quantum theory. His work on the Fermi paradox and nuclear energy has earned him the title "The Father of Nuclear Physics."

Fermi's pioneering work in particle physics and his discovery of nuclear reactions initiated the development of atomic energy. He was also instrumental in the creation of the first nuclear reactor, Chicago Pile-1, which played a pivotal role in the Manhattan Project.

Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1938 for his work on induced radioactivity, Fermi's intellectual legacy continues to influence modern physics. His work on nuclear fission and the theory of weak interactions remains a cornerstone of particle physics today.
